How it works grade → expected goals → 20,000 simulated tournaments. No maths required.

1

Every team gets a grade

We replay all 49,000 international matches since 1872 and keep a running grade for every nation: beat someone strong, it rises; lose, it falls. Spain sits top of the class today. This grade does most of the work, everything else is a small correction.

2

Grades become expected goals

One formula, the same for everyone, turns the gap between two grades into expected goals: Spain 2.1, Uruguay 0.8. Playing at home helps a lot. Two earned tilts nudge it a little: squads with heavy club legs score slightly less, and teams used to thin air do better up at altitude.

3

Then we roll loaded dice, 20,000 times

"2.1 expected goals" isn't a prediction of 2-1: it's how heavily the dice are loaded. Sometimes four go in, sometimes none, upsets happen here exactly as often as in real football. We roll dice for all 104 matches, apply FIFA's rules, and that's one possible World Cup. We play 20,000 of them and count. "Spain 25%" just means: Spain lifted the trophy in 5,000 of them.

The data diet four public files, and a list of things we refuse to know

📜 49,000 matches, 1872→today

Every official international ever played (martj42, CC0): date, teams, score, competition, venue, including the full 2026 schedule. This single file builds the team ratings, fits the goal model, and even defines the tournament structure, the 12 groups are reconstructed from the fixture graph, never typed in by hand.

⚽ 47,000 goals

Scorer, minute, penalty flag for every goal since 1916. Powers the player layer: each team's simulated goals are split among its recent scorers (time-decayed shares), producing the Golden Boot distribution below. A measured 17.3% of WC goals go to debutant scorers, that mass is reserved for "new faces".

🥅 677 shootouts

Every international penalty shootout in history. Small file, precise job: it tells us shootouts are almost, but not exactly, a coin flip. The better-rated team wins slightly more often, the de-facto home side too. Both effects are in the simulator.

🏟️ 1,248 club careers

The official squad lists (Wikipedia) joined to club-season minutes (FBref, via the Wayback Machine, the site itself blocks robots). How many minutes are in each player's legs? Feeds the fatigue tilt and the Golden Boot squad filter. Known bias, declared: minutes coverage is Europe/Big-5 only.

🚫 Deliberately excluded

Squad market values, injuries, FIFA rankings, pundit priors, betting odds. Not because they're worthless, because nothing enters this model until it proves itself in a backtest. The FIFA ranking, for one, is +0.07 log-loss worse than our Elo across World Cups 1994–2022, barely better than a coin-guess. Everything we set out to test now has a verdict: heat failed, altitude earned its place, diaspora and cohesion ended up as shadow bets (see the model lab), never silently in, never quietly dropped.

From the match file the model derives exactly three things per fixture: each team's point-in-time Elo rating, who is at home, and when it was played (older matches count less). That's the entire information diet.

How a variable gets in the admission machinery the rest of this page assumes

1

The time-machine test

Every candidate faces the same trial: go back to six past tournaments (World Cups and Euros), rebuild the forecast using only what was known before each one, and ask, would this variable have predicted them better? If out-of-sample log-loss doesn't drop, it stays out. No data later than the prediction date, no tuning on the tournament being predicted, nothing kept because it "feels right".

2

The away leg

Passing at home isn't enough. A second panel, Copa América, AFCON, Asian and Gold Cups, can't admit anything (no squad files exist there) but it can veto: if a variable only works in Europe, it's a bias, not a signal. This veto is not theoretical, it caught football capital, whose "predictive power" turned out to be Europe-only coverage, and got it removed.

3

The verdict

Four outcomes. Admitted: in the official forecast (altitude). Probation: admitted on thin evidence, first in line for demotion (fatigue). Shadow: kept out of the forecast but scored live in the lab below, so the data keeps judging it in public (capital, cohesion, diaspora). Rejected: measured, failed, documented (heat, penalty bonus, the FIFA ranking).

What earned each team its odds the pre-tournament decomposition: quality vs the draw vs venue & freshness

A snapshot of the model's eve-of-tournament reasoning, kept as it was: with the group stage decided you can read it as a scorecard of why it rated each team the way it did.

What each bar measures: a team's pre-tournament chance of finishing top two of its group, qualifying straight to the knockouts (the numbers inside the bar are percentage points that add up to that total).

How we split it: we re-run the 20,000 tournaments three ways and read off the gaps - squad quality is how the team would do in an average group (its Elo-driven strength, the draw set aside); the draw is how much its actual group is easier than average; venue & freshness is what the model's only two earned adjustments - altitude and fatigue, add or take away. A drag (the hatched piece on the right) is anything working against the team, a tougher-than-average group or tired legs, so it's drawn past the total, not added to it. The three positive pieces sum exactly to the team's real number. Pre-tournament forecast.

What earned its place the trials, every variable admitted or cut, measured by re-running the tournament without it

In one line: the model is Elo ratings → goals, plus exactly two earned adjustments, altitude and fatigue. Everything else we tried was cut: football capital, heat/climate, a penalty-taker bonus, plus diaspora and squad cohesion (kept only as "shadow bets", never in the real forecast). Heat mismatch, tried, and thrown out

Does June heat punish cold-country teams? We built the feature (venue vs home climatology, strictly point-in-time) and put it through the gate:

Also off the pitch

Penalty-taker bonus, rejected: it double-counts penalties already inside each scorer's history. Football capital (where players' clubs rank), its predictive power turned out to be a Europe-only geographic bias, so it was removed even though that cost a sliver of accuracy. Diaspora and squad cohesion can't (or barely) be backtested, they live as shadow bets in the model lab, never in the official forecast.

The match report for readers who want the formulas, not the metaphors

Two properties the cards above can't show. Every rating is point-in-time: a prediction for any date uses only matches played before it, so the backtests can't cheat. And the goal model has no per-team free parameters, five numbers shared by all 48 teams, which is why it can't overfit a single nation's story. Pre-registered backtest on World Cup 2022: log-loss 1.060 vs 1.099 for a know-nothing guess; favourites priced at 54% won 56%.
