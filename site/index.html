2 Under the hood how it's built — and how every variable earned its place

How it works grade → expected goals → 20,000 simulated tournaments. No maths required.

1

Every team gets a grade

We replay all 49,000 international matches since 1872 and keep a running grade for every nation: beat someone strong, it rises; lose, it falls. Spain sits top of the class today. This grade does most of the work — everything else is a small correction.

2

Grades become expected goals

One formula — the same for everyone — turns the gap between two grades into expected goals: Spain 2.1, Uruguay 0.8. Playing at home helps a lot. Two earned tilts nudge it a little: squads with heavy club legs score slightly less, and teams used to thin air do better up at altitude.

3

Then we roll loaded dice — 20,000 times

"2.1 expected goals" isn't a prediction of 2-1: it's the weight on a die. Sometimes four go in, sometimes none — upsets happen here exactly as often as in real football. We roll dice for all 104 matches, apply FIFA's rules, and that's one possible World Cup. We play 20,000 of them and count. "Spain 25%" just means: Spain lifted the trophy in 5,000 of them.

A 25% favourite is not a prediction that they win — it means that if this same tournament were played 20,000 times, they would lift the trophy in a quarter of them and fall short in the other three quarters. And one house rule keeps it honest: any new ingredient must prove it would have predicted past World Cups better, or it stays out. Most candidates don't make the cut. The rejects are documented below — they're the reason you can trust what's left in.

The data diet three public files — and a list of things we refuse to know

📜 49,000 matches, 1872→today

Every official international ever played (martj42, CC0): date, teams, score, competition, venue, including the full 2026 schedule. This single file builds the team ratings, fits the goal model, and even defines the tournament structure — the 12 groups are reconstructed from the fixture graph, never typed in by hand.

⚽ 47,000 goals

Scorer, minute, penalty flag for every goal since 1916. Powers the player layer: each team's simulated goals are split among its recent scorers (time-decayed shares), producing the Golden Boot distribution below. A measured 17.3% of WC goals go to debutant scorers — that mass is reserved for "new faces".

🥅 677 shootouts

Every international penalty shootout in history. Small file, precise job: it tells us shootouts are almost — but not exactly — a coin flip. The better-rated team wins slightly more often, the de-facto home side too. Both effects are in the simulator.

🏟️ 1,248 club careers

The official squad lists (Wikipedia) joined to club-season minutes (FBref, via the Wayback Machine — the site itself blocks robots). How many minutes are in each player's legs? Feeds the fatigue tilt and the Golden Boot squad filter. Known bias, declared: minutes coverage is Europe/Big-5 only.

🚫 Deliberately excluded

Squad market values, injuries, FIFA rankings, pundit priors, betting odds. Not because they're worthless — because nothing enters this model until it proves itself in a backtest. Everything we set out to test now has a verdict: heat failed, altitude earned its place, diaspora and cohesion ended up as shadow bets (see the model lab) — never silently in, never quietly dropped.

From the match file the model derives exactly three things per fixture: each team's point-in-time Elo rating, who is at home, and when it was played (older matches count less). That's the entire information diet.

What earned its place the trials — every variable admitted or cut, measured by re-running the tournament without it

In one line: the model is Elo ratings → goals, plus exactly two earned adjustments — altitude and fatigue. Everything else we tried was cut: football capital, heat/climate (tested twice), a penalty-taker bonus, plus diaspora and squad cohesion (kept only as "shadow bets", never in the real forecast). Also off the pitch

Penalty-taker bonus — rejected: it double-counts penalties already inside each scorer's history. Football capital (where players' clubs rank) — its predictive power turned out to be a Europe-only geographic bias, so it was removed even though that cost a sliver of accuracy. Diaspora and squad cohesion can't (or barely) be backtested — they live as shadow bets in the model lab, never in the official forecast.

The rule that decides it all

One referee governs the whole model: nothing enters unless it would have predicted past tournaments better. Every candidate is tested point-in-time — using only what was known before each match — on six past World Cups and Euros, and kept only if it lowers out-of-sample log-loss. No data later than the prediction date; no tuning on the tournament being predicted; nothing kept because it "feels right". It's why the list of what's in is short, why the rejects above are on the page at all — and why, when a fair version of football capital stopped helping, it came out. The discipline is the product.

Under the hood the short version — the full write-up lives in the open repo

Three layers, no black box: a from-scratch Elo rebuilt over 49,000 matches (so every rating is point-in-time — no information from the future); an Elo-driven Dixon-Coles goal model (Poisson log-rates from the Elo gap + home advantage + the low-score correction, with no per-team free parameters to overfit); and a 20,000-run Monte Carlo that samples every scoreline, applies the full FIFA tiebreakers, fills the official bracket and plays to a shootout model fitted on 677 historical shootouts. Backtest (World Cup 2022, strictly point-in-time): log-loss 1.060 vs 1.099 uniform; favourites priced at 54% won 56%.
