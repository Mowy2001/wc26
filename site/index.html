2 Under the hood how it's built, and how every variable earned its place

How it works grade → expected goals → 20,000 simulated tournaments. No maths required.

1

Every team gets a grade

We replay all 49,000 international matches since 1872 and keep a running grade for every nation: beat someone strong, it rises; lose, it falls. Spain sits top of the class today. This grade does most of the work, everything else is a small correction.

2

Grades become expected goals

One formula, the same for everyone, turns the gap between two grades into expected goals: Spain 2.1, Uruguay 0.8. Playing at home helps a lot. Two earned tilts nudge it a little: squads with heavy club legs score slightly less, and teams used to thin air do better up at altitude.

3

Then we roll loaded dice, 20,000 times

"2.1 expected goals" isn't a prediction of 2-1: it's how heavily the dice are loaded. Sometimes four go in, sometimes none, upsets happen here exactly as often as in real football. We roll dice for all 104 matches, apply FIFA's rules, and that's one possible World Cup. We play 20,000 of them and count. "Spain 25%" just means: Spain lifted the trophy in 5,000 of them.

The data diet one file does the heavy lifting; the two earned tilts eat here too

📜 49,000 matches, 1872→today

Every official international ever played (martj42, CC0). This single file is most of the model: it builds the Elo ratings, fits the goal model, reconstructs the 12 groups from the fixture graph (never typed in by hand), and feeds the live conditioning as results land. Everything below is a correction to what this file already knows.

🏔️ Venue & habitual altitudes

The elevation of every venue, and of the grounds each nation usually calls home (public elevation data). The smallest data block with the largest earned effect: teams used to thin air keep an edge at Mexico City and Zapopan, the strongest signal any candidate showed in the gate. Feeds the altitude tilt, one of the model's two earned adjustments.

🏟️ 1,248 club careers

Squad lists (Wikipedia) joined to club-season minutes (FBref, via the Wayback Machine). How much football is in each player's legs feeds the fatigue tilt, the other earned adjustment, admitted on the thinnest margin of anything in the model and now on probation with a live demotion rule. Known bias, declared: minutes coverage is Europe/Big-5 only.

🥅 677 shootouts

Every international penalty shootout in history. The smallest file with the most precise job: it says shootouts are almost, but not exactly, a coin flip, the better-rated side and the de-facto home side win slightly more often. Both effects sit in the simulator's knockout ties and nowhere else.

⚽ 47,000 goals

Scorer, minute, penalty flag for every goal since 1916. It never changes who wins, it only decides who gets the credit: each team's simulated goals are split among its recent scorers (time-decayed), and that is the whole Golden Boot market. A measured 17.3% of World Cup goals go to debutant scorers, so that mass is reserved for new faces.

🚫 Deliberately excluded

Squad market values, injuries, FIFA rankings, pundit priors, betting odds. Not because they're worthless, because nothing enters this model until it proves itself in a backtest. The FIFA ranking, for one, is +0.07 log-loss worse than our Elo across World Cups 1994–2022, barely better than a coin-guess. Everything we set out to test now has a verdict: heat failed, altitude earned its place, diaspora and cohesion ended up as shadow bets (see the model lab), never silently in, never quietly dropped.

results.csv→ point-in-time Elo→ Dixon-Coles goal rates→ 20,000 tournaments→ every team probability
goalscorers.csv→ decayed scorer shares→ goal allocation per simulation→ Golden Boot odds
squads (wiki)+ fbref minutes→ fatigue z-score→ tilt on goal rates (β=−0.028)
venue altitude+ home altitude→ acclimatisation gap→ tilt at Mexico City / Zapopan (β=0.13/km)
shootouts.csv→ shootout logit (677 obs)→ knockout ties

From the match file the model derives exactly three things per fixture: each team's point-in-time Elo rating, who is at home, and when it was played (older matches count less). That's the entire information diet.
