as a probability distribution

We don't predict who wins. We simulate the whole tournament twenty thousand times — every scoreline sampled from a statistical model of how national teams score goals — and count what happens. Every number on this page is an empirical frequency, not an opinion.

How it works three layers, each one auditable

1

Elo, rebuilt from scratch

Every international match since 1872 — 49,000 of them — replayed through the World Football Elo formula (importance-weighted K, margin multiplier, +100 home advantage). Because we compute it ourselves, we can ask for any team's rating as it was on any date: backtests never peek at the future.

2

Dixon-Coles goal model

Goals follow Poisson distributions whose rates are driven by the Elo gap, with the classic Dixon-Coles correction for 0-0 and 1-1 draws and exponential decay on old matches. No free per-team parameters — international data is too sparse for that. Backtested on World Cup 2022: log-loss 1.062 vs 1.099 for a know-nothing baseline, and well calibrated (favourites priced at 54% won 56%).

3

Tournament Monte Carlo

Each simulation samples all 72 group scorelines, applies the full FIFA tiebreaker ladder (including head-to-head), ranks the eight best third-placed teams, fills the official 32-team bracket, and plays it to the final — extra time at one-third intensity, shootouts from a model fitted on 677 historical shootouts (they're almost a coin flip). Hosts keep their home advantage wherever they genuinely play at home, knockout rounds included.

Luck is not noise to remove — it's the object of study. The Poisson sampling is football's variance: one-in-five upsets happen in one of five simulations. The model is judged only on log-loss and calibration, never on getting a single match "right".

Reading the gaps what the model doesn't know — on purpose

The USA case

The market (Kalshi) gives the USA ~51% to win group D; our model says . Not a bug: by Elo the USA is the weakest team in its own group, and home advantage alone lifts it from last to second. The market is pricing squad value, host momentum and diaspora support — signals the model will only accept when they prove themselves in a backtest (they're the next items on the roadmap).

Point-in-time discipline

Every prediction is made strictly with information available on June 11, 2026. Ratings are frozen on the eve of the opening match; hyperparameters were tuned on past World Cups, never on the data being predicted. As real 2026 results come in, they enter the simulator as fixed scorelines and every probability on this page updates.
