The 2026 World Cup,
as a probability distribution

This project is an experiment in radical data honesty: one small, public, 154-year-old dataset, every choice about what the model is allowed to know made explicit — and measured. We simulate the tournament twenty thousand times and count what happens. Every number below is an empirical frequency, and every data decision behind it has a price tag you can see.

The whole thing, in sixty seconds no maths required — the maths is one click away, everywhere

1

Every team gets a grade

We replay all 49,000 international matches since 1872 and keep a running grade for every nation: beat someone strong, it rises; lose, it falls. Spain sits top of the class today. This grade does most of the work — everything else is a small correction.

2

Grades become expected goals

One formula — the same for everyone — turns the gap between two grades into expected goals: Spain 2.1, Uruguay 0.8. Playing at home helps a lot. Two earned bonuses help a little: squads full of big-club players score slightly more, squads with heavy legs slightly less.

3

Then we roll loaded dice — 20,000 times

"2.1 expected goals" isn't a prediction of 2-1: it's the weight on a die. Sometimes four go in, sometimes none — upsets happen here exactly as often as in real football. We roll dice for all 104 matches, apply FIFA's rules, and that's one possible World Cup. We play 20,000 of them and count. "Spain 25%" just means: Spain lifted the trophy in 5,000 of them.

Like a weather forecast: 70% rain doesn't mean it will rain — it means bring an umbrella and don't be shocked by sunshine. And one house rule keeps it honest: any new ingredient must prove it would have predicted past World Cups better, or it stays out. Most candidates fail that exam. The rejects are documented below — they're the reason you can trust what's left in.

The data three public files — and a list of things we refuse to know

📜 49,000 matches, 1872→today

Every official international ever played (martj42, CC0): date, teams, score, competition, venue, including the full 2026 schedule. This single file builds the team ratings, fits the goal model, and even defines the tournament structure — the 12 groups are reconstructed from the fixture graph, never typed in by hand.

⚽ 47,000 goals

Scorer, minute, penalty flag for every goal since 1916. Powers the player layer: each team's simulated goals are split among its recent scorers (time-decayed shares), producing the Golden Boot distribution below. A measured 17.3% of WC goals go to debutant scorers — that mass is reserved for "new faces".

🥅 677 shootouts

Every international penalty shootout in history. Small file, precise job: it tells us shootouts are almost — but not exactly — a coin flip. The better-rated team wins slightly more often, the de-facto home side too. Both effects are in the simulator.

🏟️ 1,248 club careers new

The official squad lists (Wikipedia) joined to club Elo ratings (clubelo.com) and club-season minutes (FBref, via the Wayback Machine — the site itself blocks robots). Where does each player earn his living, and how many minutes are in his legs? Feeds three admitted pieces: the capital tilt, the fatigue tilt and the Golden Boot squad filter. Known bias, declared: coverage is Europe/Big-5 only.

🚫 Deliberately excluded

Squad market values, injuries, FIFA rankings, pundit priors, betting odds. Not because they're worthless — because nothing enters this model until it proves itself in a backtest. Still in the waiting room: climate, diaspora, fatigue, cohesion — one block at a time, under the same rule: improve out-of-sample log-loss or get dropped.

Methodology for the curious — the machinery behind the numbers

Layer 1 — Elo ratings, rebuilt from scratch

We replicate the World Football Elo formula (importance-weighted K factor: World Cup 60, continental finals 50, qualifiers 40, friendlies 20; goal-margin multiplier; +100 home advantage) over all 49,000 matches. Computing it ourselves, instead of scraping the published table, is what makes point-in-time queries possible: any team's rating on any date, with no information from the future. Validation: Spain #1 at 2216 on tournament eve, top-15 consistent with the reference rankings.

Layer 2 — Dixon-Coles goal model on Elo covariates

Each side's goals are Poisson with log-rates driven by the Elo gap, a home-advantage term, and the classic Dixon-Coles low-score correction (0-0 and 1-1 are more common than independence implies). No free per-team parameters — international teams play ~10 matches a year, free parameters would overfit instantly. Old matches decay exponentially (half-life ≈ 8.5 months, tuned out-of-sample). Backtest on World Cup 2022: log-loss 1.060 vs 1.099 uniform; favourites priced at 54% won 56%.

Layer 3 — Tournament Monte Carlo

Each of the 20,000 simulations samples all 72 group scorelines from the model's score matrices, applies the full FIFA tiebreaker ladder (points, goal difference, goals, head-to-head; fair play is unobservable pre-match so surviving ties break at random — equivalent to drawing lots in expectation), ranks the eight best thirds, fills the official 32-team bracket (third-place slots assigned by constraint matching against the published schedule), and plays to the final. Knockout draws go to extra time (Poisson at one-third intensity), then to a shootout model fitted on the 677 historical shootouts: P(win) = sigmoid(0.31·host + 0.68·Δelo/400). Hosts keep home advantage wherever they genuinely play at home — knockout rounds included.

Luck, and why we never grade single predictions

The Poisson sampling is football's variance: a one-in-five upset happens in one of five simulations, and that's the point. The model is evaluated exclusively on log-loss and calibration over many matches — never on whether a single pick came true. Three sources of uncertainty are treated separately: aleatoric (the sampling), epistemic-in-data (prefer expected goals where available), epistemic-in-parameters (bootstrap, on the roadmap).

Point-in-time discipline

No rating, parameter or hyperparameter ever sees data later than the prediction date. Hyperparameters are tuned on past tournaments only; ratings freeze on the eve of the opening match. This is also why the time-decay tuning result (the flat curve above) is trustworthy: it was measured strictly out-of-sample, on 345 matches across six tournaments the model had never seen.
